Moving interface problems

Numerical techniques of various kinds have emerged over last two decades for faithful computation of solutions of problems involving motion of interfaces in two- and three-dimensions. These techniques have extended the range of problems that can be numerically solved in a variety of applied fields including fluid mechanics, solid mechanics, bio-medical engineering, material science and so on. The challenge now and in the years ahead is on manipulating properties of moving interfaces and their surroundings in ways to achieve some desired objectives. The motivation comes from realization that such manipulation can dramatically change the properties of the system. The goal is then to develop multi-interface systems with potentially superior properties. We have been active in this kind of research for a while with current focus on 1) Hele-Shaw type flows and 2) porous media flows. These problems provide the scope of studying various types of eigenvalue problems whose studies require attention of researchers. They also provide an opportunity to do research in the area of nonlinear stability analysis of system of nonlinear PDEs. Ongoing research in this area also involves simulation of multi-interface flows to gain statistical understanding on mutual interaction of various interfaces and their interaction with their surroundings. Most of these problems are open problems Preprints #46, #48, #51, #52, #53 are helpful in introducing some aspects of this topic.

Background required: Knowledge at the level of ODE (Math 611), PDE (math 612), Applied Mathematics (Math641, Math 642) and numerical analysis (math 609, 610).

Multi-component multiphase flows in porous media

Several ongoing projects currently focus on various aspects of porous media flow in the context of oil recovery. Addition of various species in the displacing fluid and various flooding strategies have been extensively utilized in order to improve oil recovery. Despite considerable progress, challenges with capillary forces and fingering have prevented such flooding processes from realizing their full potential. To improve oil recovery beyond current level, a better understanding of the dependence of oil recovery on various properties of species in the displacing is required at a reasonable cost. Our current focus of several projects is about an assessment of the dependence of displacement efficiency on a variety of flooding strategy as well as for a variety of components embedded in the displacing fluid at a reasonable cost. Mathematical equations modeling this improved oil recovery process involve several field equations (system of nonlinear PDEs involving equations such as Darcy's law, continuity equations, Buckley Leverett equation, etc.) coupled with interfacial transport equations in the presence of heterogeneity, capillary forces, and some other effects. Theoretical and numerical analyses of these equations followed by numerically solving this multiscale problem are current focus of these projects. Some of the references are articles #4, #41, #57, #61, #63.

Background required: Knowledge at the level of ODE (Math 611), PDE (math 612), Applied Mathematics (Math641, Math 642) and numerical analysis (math 609, 610).

Analysis based fast algorithms in the real plane

Fast algorithms of various kinds have changed the landscape of computational science by providing an unprecedented opportunity to solve many large scale problems in reasonable time at levels of details and accuracy that were hitherto impossible. Because such algorithms attract considerable attention, there is considerable interest worldwide in reducing computational complexity of various computational processes. Any fast algorithm for a computational intensive problem of significant interest can have world wide impact. Our group has been active in this area and has contributed significantly to these efforts, particularly in Green's function representation of solutions of PDEs. The kernel of the integral in these representations is singular because of the Green's function. Traditional approaches to numerically evaluating these singular integrals are expensive and not so accurate. An analysis based fast algorithm in a disk for evaluation of such integrals was developed and tested on canonical problems by Borges and Daripa (see the article #27). This algorithm is very simple and direct. Hence it is easy to use. It is also very robust because it is not fragile, stability is not an issue, very accurate, and computational complexity is O(ln N) per node. Moreover, the constant hidden behind the order O(ln N) is so small that in practice the complexity is O(1). The algorithm is inherently parallelizable by construction and easy to use on parallel machines as well. Please see the article #27 for details. Such fast algorithms for regular regions can be used in combination with domain embedding to solve efficiently these equations in complicated domains (see articles #29, #38 and #40). Several ongoing projects focus on development and applications of analysis based fast algorithms with some ingenuity for a variety of constant and non-constant coefficient PDEs in two and three-dimensions with added emphasis on understanding ways to reduce the value of the constant hidden in O(ln N) even further. Even developing one such algorithm or reducing the value of the constant will have great impact on sciences and engineering. There are other ongoing projects on the development of a variety of reliable, fast algorithms for other applications as well. This represents a great opportunity for students interested in doing research on numerical analysis and computational science.

Background required: Knowledge at the level of ODE (Math 611), PDE (Math 612), Applied Mathematics (Math 641, Math 642) and Numerical Analysis (Math 609, 610).

Energy Resources Area (Enhanced Oil Recovery by ASP Flooding)

There is scope of doing applied and/or fundameantal and/or computational and/or mathematical modeling research in this area. There is already a group of graduate students and postdocs engaged in various aspects of this project. I am looking for more to join in this as well as in several other new areas of energy resource. Below is a brief background on the significance of this research. Read the last paragraph as well.

There is no denying the fact that there is a rising energy demand world wide in a market of tight supply. To alleviate this situation, there is going to be much needed research in energy resources area in the foreseeable future. One such area is oil recovery. After conventional (primary and secondary) oil recovery methods are exhausted, it is estimated that total residual oil in these reservoirs around the world is around 70% of the original oil in place (OOIP). This oil is retained due to capillary forces (usually after water flood in light oil reservoirs). Also oils that are less mobile or almost immobile due to high viscosity (of heavy oils and tar sands) are in a sense trapped. EOR (Enhanced Oil Recovery) methods are designed to reduce capillary forces and increase effective mobility of oils, thereby lowering the residual oil saturation and hence improving oil recovery. There are many EOR methods which can be broadly classified into three categories: (i) thermal methods; (ii) miscible methods and (iii) chemical methods. These methods differ in ways the reduction of capillary forces and enhancement of effective mobility of oil are attempted. These variety of methods are needed because reservoir formation and fluid properties determine the suitability of an EOR method for a particular reservoir. However, an EOR method which may be suitable for a reservoir from these considerations may not be economically viable for all countries at certain point in time due to excessive labor and other costs in certain countries. Ultimately, the choice of an EOR method depends on many considerations, economical as well as technological.

The percentage of OOIP retained by a reservoir after conventional flooding (if the reservoir offers favorable conditions for such flooding) depends on the type of formation. For example, conventional recovery methods work very well to recover almost 50% of OOIP for light oil leaving the rest 50% as a target of EOR. This target of EOR rises to almost 90% for heavy oil and almost 100% for tar sand. This stresses the much needed research in EOR since bulk of the OOIP is the target of EOR but only a fraction of it is recovered by currently successful EOR methods. Among thermal methods, steam based methods have been more successful commercially than others. Among non-thermal methods, miscible flooding has been remarkably successful. However, applicability of these methods is limited by the availability and cost of solvents on a commercial scale. Among immiscible gas injection methods, CO_2 (Carbon Dioxide) floods have been relatively more successful than others for heavy oils. Chemical methods have generally been uneconomic in the past, but they hold promise for the future.

One of my current research areas deals with various aspects (fundamental fluid dynamics issues, mathematical modeling, mathematical analysis of the PDE system modeling this process, Hydrodynamic stability, simulation and large scale optimization) of an EOR method by chemical flooding, namely ASP-flooding project. This project is a perfect blend of fluid mechanics, mathematics, chemistry, thermodynamics, numerical nanalysis, scientific computing and applied analysis. ASP-flooding utilizes, as the name suggests, mainly three chemicals: alkali, surfactant and polymer solutions. Surfactants are effective in lowering the interfacial tension between oil and water to a level that promotes mobilization of trapped oil drops. The alkaline agents react with the acid components of crude oil and generate in situ surfactant which overcomes the surfactant depletion in the liquid phases due to retention by the rock matrix. These three chemicals together facilitate reduction of interfacial surface tension and improvement of mobility ratio. Field tests are encouraging (recoveries are 25%-30%) but at this recovery rate, economics are marginal at best. Investment in the basic research of this EOR method by ASP-flooding (Polymer flooding is a special case of ASP-flooding) will ensure that this method has an important place in oil production for a long time to come.
